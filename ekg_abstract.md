# Engineering Knowledge Graph (EKG)

## Abstract

The artifacts of an industrial control system (hazard analyses, safety requirements specifications, FMEAs, wiring diagrams, P&IDs, PLC code, FATs, SATs, preventative maintenance procedures, and the validation matrices that tie them together) are authored separately, in different tools, by different people, over the life of a project. The information they contain is not independent. A hazard identified in the HA motivates a safety function specified in the SRS, which is implemented in PLC logic, wired through devices shown on drawings, and verified by a SAT that exercises those devices under fault conditions. The FMEA catalogs failure modes and the system reactions that address them; those reactions are implemented in hardware and software and validated by their own test procedures. Every artifact references others. The connections between them are the substance of the engineering work.

Today, those connections live primarily in the minds of the engineers who built the system. An experienced engineer can trace from a hazard through its mitigation to the code that implements it and the test that verifies it, but that trace is performed from memory, drawing on familiarity with the specific project, its history, and its naming conventions. This method works. It is also slow, labor-intensive, and difficult to delegate. When knowledge holders leave or rotate, their mental map of the project's cross-references leaves with them. Gaps in traceability, missing tests, and revision drift accumulate quietly and surface at audit, at commissioning, or after an incident.

This problem is most acute late in a project, when SAT writing, systems changes, and documentation closeout converge under a compressed timeline. Writing a SAT is not difficult in itself. The difficulty is that the information needed to write it is scattered across five or more documents that do not reference each other in a navigable way. The engineer sits with the FMEA open in one window, the drawing in another, the SRS in a third, reconstructing which device gets which failure injected, what the expected reaction is, and whether the drawing being referenced is even current. Tools exist to streamline SAT formatting and templating, but they address the writing workflow, not the information gathering problem upstream of it. Meanwhile the drawings are still revising, the FMEA is still receiving new entries as commissioning reveals gaps, and the SRS may have changed based on field conditions. The engineer is writing SATs against a moving target, with no way to confirm that the information being written from is current and consistent across the full document set.

The structure of this information is not loose or incidental. It is a graph. Hazards connect to mitigations. Mitigations connect to verifications. Failure modes connect to system reactions. Reactions connect to tests. Devices connect to drawings, to code, to procedures. The validation matrix that engineers build at the end of a project is an explicit, manual rendering of this graph: proof that the connections exist and are complete. The graph is already there. It is implicit in every cross-reference, every tag shared between a drawing and a PLC program, every FMEA row that names a device and a system reaction. The question is whether to continue navigating it from memory or to represent it as a structure that can be queried.

Treating the system design as a graph, a set of typed nodes connected by typed edges, makes a class of difficult manual problems into straightforward queries. Identifying missing SATs becomes: what verification obligations have no covering test? Identifying redundant tests becomes: which tests converge on the same physical action? Assessing the blast radius of a proposed change becomes: what other nodes are reachable from this one through cross-artifact edges? Checking whether every FMEA failure mode has a SAT that validates its detection and mitigation becomes: can the graph be traversed from the FMEA entry node to the proper SAT node? These are not new questions. Engineers ask them constantly. The graph makes them answerable without relying on any single person's memory of the project or needing to manually traverse the connections by means of four or five open documents.

The same structure carries into operations. A set of alarms firing simultaneously raises a diagnostic question: what failure modes are connected to these alarms as a group? A preventative maintenance procedure review asks: what devices are exercised by this procedure, and what other procedures share those devices? These are graph traversals over the same nodes and edges that were built during design.

Critically, none of this requires engineers to change how they work. The documents they already produce (the HAs, SRS documents, FMEAs, drawings, I/O lists, PLC source files, test procedures) are the inputs. These are highly structured, reference-rich artifacts authored in established formats. Parsing them into graph fragments is a bounded technical problem, not a research problem. Naming conventions vary between vendors, disciplines, and projects, but they are internally consistent within each entity, which is sufficient for tag resolution. The workflow is: author documents in existing tools, ingest them into the graph, query the graph for outputs. Outputs include validation reports, structured SATs, coverage analyses, blast-radius assessments, and management-of-change documentation, deliverables that are currently assembled by hand from the same underlying information.

EKG is proposed as the system that performs this ingestion, maintains the graph, and answers the queries. It meets the documentation where it already lives and returns the connective tissue of the project as a queryable structure rather than a dependency on individual expertise.
